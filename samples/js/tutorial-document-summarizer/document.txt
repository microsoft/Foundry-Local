Surfing Around Australia: A Coast-by-Coast Overview

Australia is one of the world's great surfing destinations, with more than
35,000 kilometres of coastline and reliable swell on almost every shore. The
country's geography exposes it to waves generated in both the Indian and
Pacific Oceans as well as the Southern Ocean, which means that on any given day
there is usually surf breaking somewhere. Surfing is woven into Australian
beach culture, and most coastal towns have at least one well-known break and a
local club that has been running competitions for decades.

The east coast is the most populous surfing region and arguably the most
varied. New South Wales offers everything from the long, mellow points of
Byron Bay and Crescent Head to the powerful beach breaks around Sydney's
Northern Beaches. Further north, Queensland's Gold Coast is home to a chain of
world-class right-hand points - Snapper Rocks, Greenmount, Kirra, and Burleigh
Heads - that can produce rides of several hundred metres when a cyclone swell
lines up. The water is warm for most of the year, and beginners can usually
find a sheltered corner even when the main banks are crowded.

Victoria and Tasmania, further south, are colder and more exposed. The Great
Ocean Road in Victoria includes Bells Beach, host of the world's
longest-running professional surfing event, where powerful reef waves break in
front of dramatic cliffs. Tasmania has gained a reputation in the last twenty
years for heavy slabs and big-wave spots such as Shipstern Bluff, which sit at
the cutting edge of what surfers consider rideable. Wetsuits are essential
here even in summer, and conditions can change quickly.

South Australia and Western Australia face the Indian and Southern Oceans and
receive consistent groundswell year round. Margaret River, about three hours
south of Perth, is the centre of Western Australia's surf scene and hosts
another stop on the world tour. The waves there are often large, fast, and
breaking over shallow limestone reef, which rewards experienced surfers and
keeps the lineups relatively uncrowded compared with the east coast. South
Australia's coast is more remote, with long drives between breaks, but
offers similarly powerful waves for those willing to make the trip.

The Northern Territory and the tropical top of Queensland are not traditional
surfing destinations. The Great Barrier Reef blocks most swell from reaching
the Queensland coast north of about Fraser Island, and the waters of the Top
End are better known for crocodiles than for waves. Surfers who want warm
water and consistent waves in the Australian winter usually fly to Indonesia
rather than travel north within the country.

Travel logistics are straightforward by international standards. Coastal
highways run the length of both the east and west coasts, board hire is widely
available in tourist towns, and most large airlines will carry surfboards as
checked luggage for a modest fee. Hostels and campgrounds catering to surfers
cluster around well-known breaks, and it is common to plan a multi-week trip
around a single stretch of coast, moving from spot to spot as the wind and
swell direction change.

Safety deserves serious attention. Australian beaches are patrolled by
volunteer surf lifesavers in summer, marked by the familiar red and yellow
flags, and surfers should stay outside the flagged swimming areas. Rip
currents are the leading cause of beach drownings, and learning to recognise
and escape them is a basic skill for anyone entering the water. Sharks are
present but encounters are rare; most experienced surfers regard them as a
small, manageable part of being in the ocean. Sun protection is just as
important: the Australian sun is intense, and long sessions without a rash
vest, zinc, and a hat will be felt the next day.

For a visitor with limited time, a common itinerary is to fly into Sydney,
spend a few days surfing the Northern Beaches, then drive north along the
Pacific Highway to Byron Bay and the Gold Coast over one or two weeks. Surfers
with more time, a four-wheel drive, and an appetite for solitude tend to head
west or south instead, trading crowds and warm water for empty lineups and the
sort of waves that take a lifetime to learn.
